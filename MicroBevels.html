Why use a Micro Bevel (also known as a secondary bevel)?

The sharpening process is typically multi-step :

  1. The tool is sharpened on a grindstone which is "rougher" -- a grit in the range of 600 - 1,000.
  2. The tool is then honed on a grindstone (or other media) with a much higher grit -- something in 4,000 grit and finer range (some hone on grindstones up to 13,000 grit).

The honing step can be quite onerous if the size of the surface to be honed is large (every mm2 counts). Fortunately, by increasing the angle for the honing step, the area needing to be honed can be kept small.

Micro bevels thusly are used to make sharpening (and re-sharpening) of the tool faster, getting the craftsman back to work sooner.

What Micro Bevel angle should you use?

The selection of the Micro Bevel angle is not critical for the tool's sharpness, but it does impact the time required for re-honing the tool.

  • There are some who opt for Micro Bevels which are very close to the primary bevel (a low increase) -- angles increased in the range of +1° to +2°.
  • Others opt for Micro Bevels which are a greater increase to the primary bevel -- angles increased in the area of +5°, even up to +10°.
  • I generally use +2° to +5°, with higher bevel angles on harder steels.

Want more information on honing?

There is a very good guide on honing which was written by Charles Holtzapffel titled, Turning and Mechanical Manipulation, Vol. 3 - Abrasive and Other Processes not Accomplished with Cutting Tools. This was originally published in 1850 by Charles Holtzapffel, and was later updated by Charles' son, John Jacob Holtzapffel in 1894. The 2d edition has 796 pages, of which 300 were revised from the first edition by John Jacob.
