Maintenance of this Tool in the Field

Draw Filing

Image courtesy Jordan Bunker at Make Skill Builder

Many gardening tools can be resharpened in the field using a 6" smooth cut machinist’s hand file. This is useful to carry with you. Use it to resharpen the tool as needed throughout the day.

When using the machinist’s hand file a draw stroke is a better approach as there is less chance of rounding over the edge.

Ongoing Maintenance

Be sure to remove all caked-on dirt and sap. A well-kept tool will last your lifetime, and will still be usable by your children (and maybe your grandkids).

I recommend trying to remove gunk mechanically first.

  • A wire brush or putty knife can be useful for this.
  • A green or purple scrub pad works well for removing many stuck-on materials.
  • Another recommendation is to use a product like Crean Mate. That is a small block used for cleaning resin, rust, and other gunk from your tool. It is recommended that the cleaning block be slightly wet.

Sap can be removed using a solvent. The recommended products to try are ranked below:

  1. Isopropyl alcohol works well, and is less harsh than many other options.
  2. Acetone works well, but be sure to wear protective clothing as this is not kind to your body, and be sure the area is well ventilated.
  3. Do not use petroleum distillates like lighter fluid. This just leaves a gooey mess if there are any sugars on the tool.
  4. Do NOT use bleach or trisodium phosphate. Both are oxidizers which will cause your tools to rust.

 

Biosecurity

Cleanliness of tools is key for limiting the spread of diseases. If a tool was exposed to any:

  • diseased plants,
  • soil which is infected with pests,
  • animals with diseseases, or
  • animal homes (e.g., bee hives) with diseases,

it will need to be disinfected after the tool is clean. Studies have noted these options:

70% Ethanol

or

70% Isopropyl Alcohol

 Alcohol can be used for most cleaning; however there are some severe diseases which alcohol cannot address.

Some studies have shown that rubbing alcohol may not effectively disinfect pruning tools from certain plant pathogens. For this reason, alcohol is not recommended for use in disinfecting pruning tools used on apple trees infected with fire blight. Utilize a bleach solution instead.

- Iowa State University’s Extension publication

🔥 Alcohol is flammable; be careful when using it in locations where it can ignite.
Household Bleach
(5-10% solution)		 Chlorine compounds such as bleach are easy to obtain and are and low cost. These are effective in inactivating vegetative bacteria, fungi, lipid and non-lipid viruses, Coxiella burnetii and TB.

Chlorine compounds have some effect in inactivating bacterial spores.

  • Recommended contact time: 10 minutes
  • Recommended working dilution: 5,000 ppm (1:10 dilution of household bleach)

As noted above, bleach is an oxidizer which will cause your tools to rust, especially metal which are not stainless steel. It is best to follow the use of bleach on a tool, with an alcohol wash of that tool.

More information is available at:

  1. Clean and Disinfect Gardening Tools and Containers, University of Minnesota’s Extension publication
  2. Disinfecting Your Garden Tools, University of Florida’s Institute of Food and Agricultural Sciences (IFAS) publication
  3. How Do I Sanitize My Pruning Shears?, Iowa State University’s Extension publication
  4. Sanitizing Pruning Tools, University of Arizona’s Cooperative Extension publication

Apply a Light Coat of Oil

After using any solvent, be sure to apply a thin coat of camellia oil to the tool. I like the spray bottle of camellia oil sold by Tools for Working Wood.

  • In lieu of camellia oil, jojoba oil or cedar oil are recommended by some. I've no experience with these, so I cannot recommend them. And, as camellia oil is inexpensive and readily available, it is preferred.
  • There are some who advocate using olive oil as an alternative. That is not recommended as olive oil, like all vegetable oils, are more attractive to dirt and dust, making a mess.
  • Do NOT use boiled linseed oil (BLO). Some advocate using BLO, but BLO often has heavy metals or other bad chemicals added for drying agents, and these are not good for you to handle, nor would they be good for the plants on which you might use this tool.

Tools in Storage

Tools in storage need a thin coating of camellia oil applied to all unpainted surfaces.
